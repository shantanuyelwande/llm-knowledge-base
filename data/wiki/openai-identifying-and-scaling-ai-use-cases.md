# Identifying and Scaling AI Use Cases

## Summary

A comprehensive guide from [[OpenAI]] on how organizations can systematically discover, evaluate, and implement [[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] solutions across their business. The guide provides a three-step framework for identifying high-impact use cases, teaches organizations the six fundamental AI use case primitives, and offers practical prioritization methods to maximize [[Return on Investment|ROI]] from AI investments.

---

## Overview

In just two years, 39% of U.S. adults have already used [[Artificial Intelligence|AI]]—a faster adoption rate than the [[Internet]] achieved in its first two years. According to research from [[Boston Consulting Group|BCG]], [[AI]] leaders have seen:

- **1.5x** faster revenue growth
- **1.6x** higher shareholder returns  
- **1.4x** better return on invested capital

than their less advanced peers.

However, only 1% of companies surveyed by McKinsey believe their [[AI]] investments have reached full maturity, indicating a significant gap between investment and realized value.

---

## Three Core Principles

Successful AI implementation depends on three foundational principles:

### 1. Leadership-Led Adoption

[[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] should be led and encouraged by organizational leadership to ensure sustained commitment and resource allocation.

### 2. Simplicity Over Complexity

Complex use cases often slow progress. Empowering employees to find practical use cases that work best for their specific roles is often a faster path to success than pursuing elaborate solutions.

### 3. Community-Driven Discovery

Encouraging adoption through [[Hackathons|hackathons]], use case workshops, and peer-led learning sessions catalyzes discovery across the organization.

---

## Step 1: Identifying Opportunities for AI Impact

The first step is finding parts of your business that can be immediately improved with [[Artificial Intelligence|AI]]. Think of AI as creating "super-assistants" for your workforce—tools that never get tired, lose focus, or become unavailable.

AI adds the most value in three key areas:

### Repetitive, Low-Value Tasks

Every job involves tedious, manual tasks that take people away from strategic work. These mindless tasks slow [[Productivity|productivity]] and make work frustrating and unfulfilling.

**Examples:**
- Summarizing meeting notes
- Searching for trends in spreadsheet data
- Generating product requirements documents
- Answering repetitive questions

**Case Study - Launch Darkly:** Chief Product Officer Claire Vo created an "Anti To-Do List" of tasks she no longer focuses on, including monitoring KPIs, tracking competitors, and sharing customer stories via [[Slack]]. Her philosophy: "Every time I do something I find annoying, I ask myself, how can I not have to do this again?"

### Skill Bottlenecks

Work often slows when employees reach the limits of their expertise and need input from other departments or specialists. This creates bottlenecks and delays progress.

[[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] can help bridge these gaps by helping employees expand their skillsets—from [[Data Analysis|data analysis]] and trend visualization to [[Coding|coding]]—without waiting for additional support. Expert teams get more time back from lower-value cross-functional tasks, and everyone learns to communicate in each other's language.

### Navigating Ambiguity

Knowledge work often involves ambiguity and open-ended challenges. Employees may struggle to get started or become blocked, causing projects to stall.

[[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] acts as a catalyst, helping to:
- Generate ideas
- Analyze data
- Propose next steps when the path is unclear

**Case Study - Marketing Team:** Marketing teams across organizations use [[ChatGPT]] voice mode to brainstorm campaign ideas and unblock creativity, helping teams move from concept to execution faster.

## Step 2: Teaching Teams the Six AI Use Case Primitives

Once organizations understand where [[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] adds value, the next step is training teams on the fundamental ways they can use AI. Analysis of over 600 customer use cases reveals that most fall into six "primitives"—fundamental use case types applicable across all departments and disciplines.

### 1. Content Creation

[[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] supports content creation across all teams, whether summarizing sales calls or generating first drafts of strategy documents, blog posts, web pages, and even images and visualizations.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Automatically write in your company's style and tone
- Follow preferred document structures
- Provide writing feedback
- Translate work into different languages
- Repurpose content for different audiences and channels

**Department-Specific Use Cases:**

**Marketing:**
- Create campaign strategies, headlines, and email campaigns
- Generate content outlines and first drafts
- Repurpose content for different audiences or channels

**Finance:**
- Create draft policy documents and technical accounting memos for expert review

**Product:**
- Build product requirements documents
- Generate product descriptions, release notes, and launch communications
- Create user guides

**Sales:**
- Generate account plans and call scripts
- Draft follow-up emails

**Case Study - Premaqa:** The life sciences company saved 135 hours in their first six months using [[ChatGPT]] Enterprise for first-draft email campaigns. They also generate campaign briefs from message documents and translate copy into paid ads for specific marketing channels.

### 2. Research

[[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] is widely used for research across industries, from quick learning about new concepts to comprehensive, multi-step research projects that scan the web for articles, data points, and insights.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Search the web for relevant articles and competitive data
- Upload long internal documents for quick insights
- Specify format and structure of analysis (tables, bullet points, organized sections)
- Follow detailed instructions with attention to detail

**Department-Specific Use Cases:**

**Sales and Marketing:**
- Investigate new industries
- Understand competitors better
- Research new audiences

**Finance:**
- Search for benchmarks from publicly listed companies
- Identify M&A targets
- Research accounting standards and guidance

**Product:**
- Size new markets
- Research competitors
- Identify trends
- Analyze user feedback

**IT:**
- Search for new vendors
- Rate vendor products' strengths and weaknesses

**Software Engineering:**
- Review API endpoints
- Explore external documentation

#### Deep Research

[[Deep Research]] is a new agentic capability in [[ChatGPT]] that conducts multi-step research on the internet independently. Given a prompt, it finds, analyzes, and synthesizes hundreds of online sources to create a comprehensive report at the level of a research analyst.

### 3. Coding

Many [[Software Engineering|software engineers]] are power users of [[Artificial Intelligence|AI]]. They use it for:
- Debugging code
- Generating first-draft code in unfamiliar languages
- Porting code from one language to another
- "Rubber-ducking" their code (explaining code to find errors)

In the past two years, [[Artificial Intelligence|AI]]'s capabilities in mathematics, science, and coding across many languages have significantly improved, with many tools now offering real-time code previews.

Non-coders are also taking up coding with AI assistance. Marketers and finance teams can use natural language to build [[Python]] scripts to automate processes, [[SQL]] queries to retrieve data, or visualizations with front-end code for websites or internal presentations.

**Department-Specific Use Cases:**

**Software Engineers:**
- Debug or rubber duck code
- Port code to other languages
- Research API endpoints

**Marketing:**
- Build interactive charts and data visualizations
- Write [[SQL]] for data analysis

**Finance:**
- Create [[Python]] scripts to automate parts of the monthly close

**Product:**
- Build interactive prototypes to quickly flesh out new product ideas

**Case Study - Tinder:** Tinder's engineering team uses [[ChatGPT]] to generate first-draft syntax when working with non-intuitive languages like Bash scripts that require specialized knowledge. [[ChatGPT]] improves their coding efficiency, making it easy to reference and query external API documentation and troubleshoot architectural decisions.

### 4. Data Analysis

[[Artificial Intelligence|AI]] helps anyone harmonize data from different sources, identify insights and trends, and work with complex spreadsheet data without needing advanced [[Excel]], [[SQL]], or [[Python]] skills.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Provide AI with multiple spreadsheets or screenshots of dashboards
- Interpret spreadsheet data and understand visual charts
- Help format output for reporting
- Guide how results are structured (chart types, summary formats, comparison logic)

- Summarize key trends from a dashboard screenshot

- Analyze trends and social media feedback
- Upload CRM data on feature requests to surface new opportunities
